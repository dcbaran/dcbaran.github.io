Angell Hall

Entrances

Inside

Entrances

Angell Hall has one accessible entrance on the left side of the main staircase. On the right side of the staircase, there is a ground-level door that from a distance looks accessible due to a small blue sign with the international symbol of accessibility on it, but upon viewing the door more closely, a visitor can see that there is a small step leading up to the door, and the accessibility sign actually has an arrow under it that points to the right. The arrow points directly at a wall. The large, grand staircase is not accessible for those with physical disabilities, but a small blue sign points to the left (this time, in the correct direction) indicating that there is an accessible entrance to the left of the staircase.

A ramp leads down to the accessible entrance, which requires both a keycard scan and the pressing of a button to enter the building.

Chemistry Building

Entrances

Entrances

The Chemistry Building has several accessible entrances, some that are confusingly marked with a sign of accessibility but don’t possess any features that make them accessible (such as a button that opens the door or an automatic motion-sensing door), and some that look accessible from a distance but actually have a small arrow indicating that the door marked with the sign is not the actual accessible door.

The entrance facing the Michigan League is the only entrance that actually has a button that will open the door when pushed.

Dana Building

Entrances

Entrances

The Dana Building has a staircase in the diag entrance, which means it is inaccessible for those who might want to access the building but are in a wheelchair or have another physical disability. A door on the left side of the building has a sign indicating it is accessible, and a button to open the door. However, there is no keycard to scan student/faculty/staff MCards, so this door is simply locked on the weekend.

In the back of the building, there is a door with both a keycard scanner and a button to open the door. The door, however, is a fair distance from both the button and the keycard, so the visitor must hurry if they want to get inside before the door closes.

East Hall

Entrances

Entrances

East Hall has two entrances on the side facing the diag that are not accessible due to stairs. The visitor must circle all the way around the building to the entrance on Church St., which is accessible and has both a keycard scanner and a button to open the doors.

Hatcher Graduate Library

Entrances

Entrances

Hatcher Graduate Library is not accessible from the front, despite having a set of ramps on either side of its large, front staircase. The ramps are too steep to be used by wheelchairs, so there are two blue signs indicating that the ramps are not accessible. Using the same sign and color for a sign that indicates inaccessibility could be confusing for anyone trying to enter the building who is looking for an accessible entrance.

The visitor must circle all the way around to the back of the building, once again passing an entrance with a staircase on their way.

The back entrance is the accessible one, where the visitor can find a keycard and a button to open the doors and enter the building.

Randall Laboratory

Entrances

Entrances

Randall Laboratory has several ground-level entrances, but only one is accessible, meaning it is equipped with a button and keycard scanner that will open the door automatically. There is additionally an wheelchair lift behind this door, next to the staircase.

In the arch to the diag that is a part of Randall, there is a confusingly placed, thick, cylindrical concrete post in the center of the walkway. This could make the space more difficult to navigate for someone who has trouble squeezing through small spaces. And if a visitor is coming from the diag, they have to get through this space to get to the accessible entrance.

Shapiro Undergraduate Library

Entrances

One of the undergraduate library’s main entrances is an automatic door. There is no button or keycard scanner (the building is open 24 hours and security checks visitor MCards after a certain time of night). The library employed a strategy of simply installing motion-sensing automatic doors rather than forcing the visitor to press a button before accessing the building.

The University might consider installing similar doors to buildings with classrooms, that could open automatically on weekdays, at least.

Tisch Hall’s main entrance i accessible, with the keycard scanner in close proximity to the button that automatically opens the door to the building.

There are a few other doors into the building off to the side, but none of these are accessible despite being ground-level entrances.

University of Michigan Museum of Art

Entrances

Entrances

All of the ground-level entrances to UMMA are accessible. The older section of UMMA is not accessible due to the staircase in front of the entrance. There is a blue sign with the sign of accessibility pointing to the left to indicate that there are accessible entrances that way, but the sign was temporarily obscured by a different sign that advertised the current exhibit at the museum, which could potentially cause confusion.

Weiser Hall

Entrances

Entrances

Weiser Hall has an entrance that is marked with a blue accessibility sign but is not actually accessible. Someone took the liberty of drawing an arrow pointing to the left underneath the sign with chalk to indicate that the accessible entrances are to the left of this entrance.

The other side of the building has an entrance that is actually accessible, and is correctly marked as such. The entrance is equipped with a keycard scanner and a button to open the door automatically.

Additionally, the entrance to the newly renovated ground floor area of Weiser also comes equipped with a button that will open the door automatically when pushed. There is not keycard scanner, and the doors are locked on the weekends. The buttons do not work when the doors are locked.

West Hall

Entrances

Entrances

None of the entrances to West Hall are marked as accessible. The entrances are only equipped with keycards, if they are equipped with anything. One of the doors is marked with a poster with a small section about accessibility, reading “for problems related to a disability, Please contact the ADA Coordinator at 763-0235,” complete with the international sign of accessibility.
